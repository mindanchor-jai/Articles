I

The First Thread · Long Before Manthara

The Curse of Shravana's Father
— The Seed Planted in Darkness

Every story has a beginning that most people never read.

Long before Manthara whispered a single word into Queen Kaikeyi's ear, the Ramayana had already been written by forces far beyond human scheming. The story begins not in the palace of Ayodhya — but in a dark forest, beside a quiet river, on a moonlit night.

King Dasharatha — young, skilled, proud — was practicing the art of Shabdabhedi: hunting by sound alone. He heard a sound in the darkness near the river. He released his arrow. The sound was not an animal.

It was Shravana Kumar — a devoted son, carrying his blind, elderly parents in two baskets balanced on his shoulders on a pilgrimage to the holy tirthas. He had stopped to collect water for them. The arrow struck. And as Shravana died, he thought not of himself — but of his parents, still waiting in their baskets, thirsty, unaware.

The Blank Filled

Shravana Kumar was carrying his parents on his shoulders to the Pushkar Tirtha — the sacred lake of Lord Brahma in Rajasthan — for a holy pilgrimage. This act of devotion was so pure that Shravana is celebrated as the ideal of filial duty (Pitru Bhakti) in the Hindu tradition.

Dasharatha went to the river. He gave the dying boy water. He then carried that water to the blind parents and confessed what he had done. The parents wept — not just in grief, but in cosmic grief. And then the father, a great sage, cursed King Dasharatha:

"As we die in the absence of our son, so shall you also die — in anguish — in the absence of yours."

This was not anger. This was cosmic law being spoken aloud. The sage was not merely a grieving father — he was a vessel through which the universe registered what justice required. The seed of the Ramayana was planted in that moment. Everything else — every character, every event — grew from this single root.

Now ask yourself: where is Manthara in this story? She hadn't been born yet. And yet the curse already demanded exactly what she would one day arrange.

II

The Vessel · Not the Villain

Manthara — The Instrument
the Universe Chose

She did not change destiny. She delivered it.

History — and most retellings — position Manthara as the villain of the Ramayana. The crooked maid. The jealous schemer. The woman who robbed Ayodhya of its rightful king and set in motion fourteen years of suffering.

But step back. Look at the entire architecture of what was already in place before she spoke a single word.

The curse demanded Dasharatha die in the absence of his sons. Two boons existed — unused — for exactly this moment. Bharat was conveniently, inexplicably absent. The Vanara army was already waiting in the forest Rama would pass through. Ravana's boon had a gap — human and monkey — that would only matter if Rama was on the ground, in exile, moving through southern India.

The Word to Fill

Manthara was not a manipulator. She was a Nimitta — a Sanskrit word meaning an instrument, an agent, a cause that carries out what has already been decided at the cosmic level. In the Bhagavad Gita, Lord Krishna uses the same word for Arjuna: "You are merely the nimitta. The war has already been won."

Manthara could only use what was already there. She could not create new boons. She could not make Bharat absent — he already was. She could not manufacture a universe in which Rama needed to be in the forest — that need was already established by Ravana's very existence.

She moved the pieces exactly as far as they needed to move. Not one inch further. After Rama left, Manthara disappears from the story. Because her role was done. Precisely done. The universe had no more use for her.

The so-called manipulator can only manipulate as far as the situation requires — and not one moment, not one step beyond it.

III

The Mahabharata · The Second Epic

Shakuni Mama — The Dice
That Karma Loaded

He did not create the war. He revealed what was already inevitable.

The Mahabharata is not a war story. It is a karma story. The war was not created by Shakuni — it was the culminating consequence of centuries of choices, debts, wrongs, and cosmic debts that had to be settled.

The Kuru dynasty carried within it seeds of its own destruction: the oath of Bhishma that bound him to silence at critical moments; the curse on the Pandavas' lineage; Duryodhana's deep-rooted jealousy which existed from birth, before Shakuni ever rolled a single die.

Shakuni was clever. Cunning. His dice were loaded with intention. But what intention? What could he actually achieve? He could only make Duryodhana's existing greed execute itself. He could only accelerate what was already present. The Pandavas had to go through their own exile. The war had to happen. The debts of the entire Kuru family — across generations — demanded a reckoning.

Shakuni didn't create Duryodhana's jealousy. He was simply the instrument through which it expressed itself completely — so that destiny could be paid in full.

And when the war was over? Shakuni was gone. His purpose complete. The Pandavas returned. Dharma was restored. Every player in the Mahabharata — including its greatest villain — served the exact purpose the cosmic order required of them. Not more. Not less.

The Deeper Pattern

In both epics, the so-called "manipulators" appear at exactly the moment they are needed, execute their role with precision, and then fade from the story. They do not linger. They do not achieve anything beyond what was cosmically required. They are not authors of destiny — they are its hands.

The suffering was always part of the path — they just triggered the timing

A manipulator can only manipulate exactly as much as the situation requires — and not one breath more. That is not power. That is servitude to a plan they cannot even see.

This does not mean we excuse harmful behaviour. It means we expand our understanding of how the universe works. When something painful happens to you — when someone manipulates a situation against you — before you collapse into victimhood, ask yourself:

What if this was always supposed to happen? What if this is the forest before the kingdom? What if the manipulator in my story is simply the arrow that releases me from the life I was too comfortable to leave on my own?

Rama had to go to the forest. Not because Manthara was evil. But because Ravana needed to die, Lanka needed to fall, and a king who had only known palaces could never have found the Vanara army, crossed the ocean, and done what only a human who had walked through exile could do.

The Universe Needs

Every person who ever "manipulated" your life — look again.

Rama ruled Ayodhya. He ruled for the better part of his life — as king, as husband, as the ideal man. The kingdom always belonged to him. Manthara could not take that away, because it was never hers to take. She could only delay it — by exactly as many years as the universe required.

The Pandavas reclaimed Hastinapur. The Mahabharata war settled every karmic debt the dynasty had accumulated. The manipulators played their part. And when the part was done — destiny fulfilled itself, completely, on time, without error.

Manipulators can only manipulate exactly as much as the situation requires — not one step more, not one breath beyond. They are not authors of your destiny. They are instruments of it. And when their role is done, the kingdom was always going to be yours.
