The Colonization That Stayed
After Independence

✦

In 1947, India got its country back.
The flags changed. The administrators left.
The maps were redrawn.
But something stayed.
Something that didn't need a passport to remain.

Political independence is an event. It has a date, a ceremony, a moment you can point to and say: this is when it changed. Psychological independence is different. It has no ceremony. It requires no signing. And it does not arrive simply because the external authority has been removed. Because by the time the British left India in 1947, the deeper project — the reshaping of how Indians understood themselves, their worth, and their place in the hierarchy of the world's civilisations — was already self-sustaining. It no longer needed external enforcement. It had become internal.

The colonisers left.
But they left the operating system running.

This is what psychological colonisation means. Not that you hate yourself. Not that you consciously reject your heritage. But that the standards by which you measure your own worth — your intelligence, your achievements, your culture's legitimacy — were set by a system that positioned itself above you. And you absorbed those standards so thoroughly, over so many generations, that they now feel like your own judgement rather than an inherited one.

What It Actually Looks Like in Your Life

This is the part of the conversation that most people skip. They will acknowledge the historical argument. They will agree that colonialism caused damage. And then they will move on, as though the agreement is the work. It is not. The work is in the recognition of where this lives in you specifically. Not in the abstract. In your actual daily behaviour.

  • ◈ You feel prouder after foreign validationYou practise yoga. You know its depth. But when a Western celebrity talks about how it changed their life, you feel a specific kind of satisfaction — as though your practice has now been confirmed as something real. You didn't need that confirmation. But it felt good anyway.
  • ◈ You apologise for your traditions before sharing themWhen you explain a ritual, a belief, or a practice to someone outside your culture, you prepend it with distance: "I know this sounds strange, but…" or "It's a cultural thing, don't worry about it." You translate your heritage into apology before anyone has asked you to.
  • ◈ English is still the language of intelligenceIn most Indian professional and academic contexts — perhaps in your own internal monologue — English is the language in which serious thinking happens. Your mother tongue is for home, for warmth, for family. Not for the boardroom, the conference, the credential. That hierarchy was not natural. It was manufactured.
  • ◈ A foreign degree carries different weightYou know, intellectually, that the quality of education depends on the institution and the student. And yet — not just in your family, but in most of the systems around you — a degree from abroad carries an almost automatic prestige that its domestic equivalent has to earn in a different way. That differential did not come from evidence. It came from conditioning.

I spent three years working in a field deeply rooted in Indian thought, and I watched myself constantly translating its concepts into Western academic language before I felt comfortable presenting them to peers. Not because the translation improved the idea. Because it made me feel like I had the right to be taken seriously.

You were not born with a hierarchy.
You were handed one and told it was common sense.

How the Post-Independence Elite Became the New Gatekeepers

Here is the part that complicates the narrative — and needs to be said honestly. After 1947, the people who took over India's institutions were largely those educated in colonial schools, trained in colonial universities, shaped by colonial frameworks. They were not bad people. Many were brilliant, dedicated, and genuinely committed to the nation's development. But they had been formed by a specific educational culture — and that culture carried its own assumptions about what modernity looked like, what progress required, and which ways of knowing were serious.

The result: the colonial prestige hierarchy was not dismantled at independence. It was inherited by the new leadership — and maintained, now entirely from the inside. English-medium education became aspirational. Western-style planning and governance became the template for development. The institutions that shaped you were built and run by people who had themselves absorbed the lesson that Western = advanced and traditional = backwards, and who — with the best intentions — passed that lesson forward.

This is not about blame. It is about understanding the mechanism clearly enough to see where it still operates. Because it still does. In you. In your family's aspirations for you. In the institutions that evaluated you. In the quiet, persistent sense that you need to prove your intelligence in a particular language, through a particular kind of credential, against a particular kind of standard.

"Independence freed the nation.
It could not, by itself,
free the mind that had learned
to doubt its own worth."

I've had conversations with my parents about this. Both highly educated, both deeply proud of being Indian. Both also, without realising it, using "Western" and "international" interchangeably — as though they were the same thing. That conflation is not a mistake. It is the inheritance.

You're not confused.
You were conditioned.
And conditioning this deep
doesn't announce itself —
it feels like your own opinion.

The beginning of undoing this is not dramatic. It does not require you to reject Western thought or to swing into uncritical cultural nationalism. It requires something quieter: the willingness to notice, in specific moments, when you are applying a borrowed standard to yourself — and to ask whether you would choose that standard if you were choosing freely. That noticing is the first act of genuine psychological sovereignty. And something is beginning to shift in exactly that direction.

Leading Into Episode 5

Yoga is returning. Ayurveda is returning. Ancient texts are being studied again, in new ways, by a new generation. Something is genuinely shifting. But the question worth asking — honestly — is whether we're reclaiming these things because we've done the interior work, or because the world has finally told us they're worth reclaiming.

That question is the entire next chapter.
