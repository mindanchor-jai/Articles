You Didn't Lose Your Identity.
It Was Redesigned.

✦

You know your festivals. Your food. Your language.
You'd call yourself connected to your culture.
And yet — quietly, without being able to explain why —
you measure all of it against something else.

You didn't choose that measurement. You didn't wake up one day and decide that Western frameworks were the standard and everything Indian needed to justify itself against them. That hierarchy was handed to you — embedded so gradually, through so many ordinary channels, that by the time you could think critically about it, it already felt like common sense.

That's the point. That was always the point.

Most people feel something is off.
Almost no one can name exactly what.

The unnamed feeling — the low, persistent hum of cultural self-doubt dressed as open-mindedness — is not a personality flaw. It is not confusion. It is the precise, predictable output of a system designed almost two centuries ago and still running. The British colonial project did not just occupy land and extract wealth. Its deeper, more lasting work was interior. It occupied the criteria by which you judge what is worth knowing, worth respecting, worth being proud of.

And it did this without announcing itself. It arrived as education. As modernity. As uplift. You didn't feel colonised. You felt upgraded. That distinction is everything.

I used to think that honouring my roots meant defending them in arguments. It took me years to realise the real work was quieter than that — noticing the moments I felt embarrassed by them before anyone else had said a single word.

The Mechanism — How It Was Actually Installed

Take language. You were educated in English, or told that English education was categorically superior to regional alternatives. Not because English is inherently richer than your mother tongue. But because fluency in English was a signal of administrative usefulness to a colonial government. That utility became prestige. That prestige became aspiration. And that aspiration — passed down through families who genuinely wanted the best for their children — became the invisible assumption you now live inside: that to be articulate is to speak English, that to think rigorously is to cite Western academic sources.

Your mother tongue — which carries its own philosophy, its own logic, its own centuries of refined thought — became a home language. Warm. Private. But somehow less serious than the language your ambition lives in.

You didn't decide that.
It was decided for you. Before you were born.

The same architecture runs through every institution that shaped you. Traditional medicine was framed as pre-scientific. Indigenous knowledge systems were framed as curiosity rather than scholarship. Oral histories were framed as myth rather than record. Spiritual practices were framed as superstition rather than sophisticated inquiry into consciousness. Each framing was a quiet vote — repeated across textbooks, classrooms, newspapers, films, and the language of polite educated conversation — for a hierarchy you were never asked to consent to.

Because the hierarchy was embedded in the institutions you were taught to trust — schools, universities, media — you absorbed it as neutral reality. You weren't told your culture was inferior. You were simply never adequately shown what it actually contained. The absence did the work that direct argument could not.

"You didn't reject your roots.
You were never fully shown what they held.
The edit happened before you could read the original."

What Internalised Inferiority Actually Looks Like

Here's where you need to be honest with yourself. Because this doesn't look like self-hatred — that would be easy to spot and reject. Internalised cultural inferiority is far subtler. It looks like this: you explain a traditional practice to someone and you lead with its Western scientific validation, not its own internal logic. You feel a specific kind of pride in your culture's achievements only after a foreign institution confirms them. You are more fluent in critiquing your traditions than in understanding them deeply. You call certain customs "just something we do" — a phrase that does enormous work, quietly dismantling the need to understand why.

You love your culture. But you hold it slightly at arm's length when anyone is watching.

That arm's length is not yours. It is borrowed. And it has been borrowed for so long that it feels like your own posture.

I remember explaining meditation to a colleague once and immediately citing a Harvard study on cortisol reduction. Not because they asked for evidence. Because somewhere in me, I still needed the external authority to make the practice feel serious enough to mention out loud.

The most effective colonisation never needed force.
It needed you to adopt the coloniser's standards
as your own —
and then enforce them on yourself.

Seeing this is not about blame. Not about grievance. It is about accuracy — because you cannot build a genuinely whole identity on a foundation you have never examined. And you cannot examine what you cannot yet name. This article is the naming. The next chapter goes into the machinery — the specific institution that reached you first, shaped you most, and did its work so thoroughly you still don't fully see its edges.

Leading Into Episode 2

The confusion didn't start in your head. It started in a classroom. In a curriculum designed not to make you wise — but to make you useful. Useful to a system that was never built around you.

The next chapter is about what was there before the classroom. And what it cost you when it was replaced.
