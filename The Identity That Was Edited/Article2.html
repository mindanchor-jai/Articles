The Day Learning Stopped
Being Understanding

✦

Think about everything you studied in school.
Now ask yourself: how much of it did you actually understand —
versus how much did you learn to reproduce
well enough to pass?

You are not the problem. You never were. The system you were handed was not built to produce thinkers. It was built to produce outputs — people who could perform well within a defined structure, fill designated roles, and not ask too many questions about the structure itself. That system has a name, a history, and a specific author. And understanding it is not an academic exercise. It is the most direct explanation for why you feel capable but somehow disconnected from your own intelligence.

You were given an education.
Nobody asked if it was yours.

What the Gurukul Actually Was — and Why It Matters Now

Before the colonial school system arrived, India had an educational philosophy called the Gurukul. You may have heard the name. You probably weren't taught what it actually meant — and that omission is itself part of the story.

The Gurukul was not a building. It was a relationship. A student lived with a teacher — the Guru — for years, sometimes a decade or more. Learning was not divided into subjects and periods. It was total immersion in a way of thinking, being, and seeing the world. The student studied astronomy, mathematics, philosophy, medicine, and statecraft — but not as separate disciplines. As facets of a single integrated understanding of reality.

Here is what you need to sit with: Takshashila, one of the world's earliest universities, flourished in ancient India from at least the 5th century BCE. It drew students from Persia, Greece, China, and Arabia. It taught over sixty subjects. Chanakya — the political strategist behind the Mauryan empire — studied and taught there. Panini formalised Sanskrit grammar there with a precision so advanced that 20th-century computational linguists would draw from it when designing programming languages. This was not primitive. This was one of the most sophisticated intellectual cultures the world had ever produced.

And then it was replaced. Gradually. Systematically. By something that looked like progress.

I went through twelve years of schooling and never once heard Aryabhata's name in a science class. I learned Newton's laws in the same year I should have learned that an Indian mathematician had calculated the value of pi and the length of the solar year over a thousand years before Newton was born. That gap wasn't accidental.

The colonial classroom didn't destroy Indian knowledge.
It made you a stranger to it.

In 1835, Thomas Babington Macaulay submitted his famous Minute on Indian Education to the British government. The document is worth reading in full — not for its historical curiosity, but because it describes, with unusual clarity, exactly what was done to you. Macaulay argued that a single shelf of a good European library was worth the whole native literature of India and Arabia. He proposed replacing the existing educational system — rooted in Sanskrit, Arabic, and indigenous scholarship — with English-medium instruction centred on Western thought.

His stated goal: to form a class of persons Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect.

Read that again. Not educated Indians. Not informed Indians. Indians who would think in English, value what the English valued, and be useful to a colonial administration that needed local intermediaries. The curriculum was not designed to develop you. It was designed to position you.

You inherited the second column. And here's what makes it particularly insidious: the 10+2 model is so deeply embedded now that questioning it feels radical. You were not just taught within this system. You were taught to trust it as the natural shape of how learning works. The Gurukul was reframed as quaint or regressive. The Western structure was framed as universal.

It is not universal. It is a specific choice made for specific reasons — and those reasons had nothing to do with your development and everything to do with a colonial administration's labour requirements.

I remember the first time I read about the Nalanda university — its library of nine million manuscripts, its international student body, its destruction in the 12th century. I felt something I couldn't name at the time. I think it was grief. Not for the building. For the version of intellectual life I had never been told was possible.

"You studied to pass.
You were never taught to question
what was worth passing."

How Education Controls Perception — Not Just Knowledge

Education does not only fill minds with information. It calibrates the standard by which you decide what counts as information worth having. A system that systematically omits indigenous contributions to science, philosophy, mathematics, and medicine does not produce neutral minds. It produces minds with a shaped blind spot — a curated absence at the centre of their self-understanding.

You can name what you were taught. You cannot as easily name what you were never shown existed. That gap — between what you know and what you were allowed to know — is where cultural identity quietly erodes. Not through rejection. Through unawareness.

Think about what this means practically. You grew up with a vague sense that India had a rich history. But you were not taught its specific intellectual achievements with the same rigour and pride that British history was presented. You were not taught that Indian mathematicians invented the binary number system that your computer runs on. You were not taught that Indian physicians were performing cataract surgery fifteen centuries before Europe had a word for the procedure. You were not given the tools to have the same instinctive confidence in your own civilisation's intellectual legacy that a Western-educated person has in theirs.

That asymmetry was not accidental. And it is not ancient history. It is the foundation your present self-understanding was built on.

You weren't educated.
You were formatted —
to be useful to a system
that never asked what was useful to you.

Leading Into Episode 3

But here is the part that goes even deeper. The education system shaped what you were allowed to know. What about the knowledge itself — what happened to it? Not all of India's intellectual heritage was buried in the curriculum. Some of it took a different route entirely.

It was absorbed. Reattributed. Returned to you under a different name, with a different author, from a different country. The next chapter is about that specific act — and why you still feel its effects every time you trust a Western source over your own tradition.
