Micro-Article 03  /  Erasure → Rebranding

It Wasn't Erased.
It Was Renamed.

✦

What if the discoveries you call Western
were never originally Western?
What if you've been using your own inheritance —
under someone else's name?

History is not simply a record of what happened. It is a record of what was deemed worth remembering — and by whom. The civilisation that controls the writing of history controls far more than the past. It controls the present self-understanding of every culture whose story gets told through someone else's lens. For India, that lens was held, for nearly two centuries, by a colonial power with its own reasons for what to illuminate and what to cast into shadow.

What happened to India's intellectual heritage was not wholesale erasure. Erasure would have been cleaner. What actually occurred was something more psychologically durable: selective absorption and reattribution. Knowledge was taken from its original context, stripped of its cultural roots, renamed, and returned to the world under foreign authorship.

What Was Relocated

Mathematics

"Arabic numerals" and the decimal system

The numeral system, including zero as a mathematical concept, was developed in ancient India — most notably by Brahmagupta — and transmitted to Europe via Arab scholars. The debt to India was dissolved in transit.

Astronomy

The Earth as a sphere; heliocentric observations

Aryabhata, in the 5th century CE, proposed the Earth's rotation and calculated the solar year with extraordinary precision — centuries before Copernicus. These observations were marginalised in the dominant historical narrative.

Medicine & Surgery

Advanced surgical procedures, plastic surgery

Sushruta's Compendium, written around 600 BCE, describes surgical instruments, procedures including rhinoplasty, and ethical frameworks for medical practice that anticipate modern principles by millennia.

Linguistics & Logic

Formal grammar, computational logic structures

Panini's Ashtadhyayi, composed around the 4th century BCE, is a formal grammar of Sanskrit so precise that modern computer scientists have drawn from its rule-based architecture in designing programming languages.

The Psychology of Credibility

The deeper problem is not the historical record. It is what the distorted record did to the psychology of credibility. When a tradition is repeatedly omitted from the texts considered authoritative, a perception takes hold: this tradition did not produce anything worth recording. That perception becomes self-reinforcing — the absence of recognition creates the assumption of absence.

This is why, today, many Indians feel a pull to validate indigenous practices through external recognition. Yoga becomes more credible when Harvard studies it. Ayurveda gains legitimacy when Western pharmaceutical companies begin researching its compounds. Turmeric, used in Indian households for generations, becomes newsworthy as a "superfood" when it appears in a Western journal. None of this is irrational. It is the predictable output of a long-conditioned trust hierarchy — where external validation precedes self-recognition.

You doubt your own legacy…
while using it daily.
The doubt wasn't yours. It was installed.

Reclaiming authorship of one's own intellectual heritage is not about nationalistic pride or historical grievance. It is about restoring an accurate relationship between a people and their own capacity. When a civilisation understands the depth of what it has produced, it does not need to borrow confidence from foreign endorsement. The knowledge was always there. The question was only who got to name it.

Leading Into Episode 4

But knowing that the knowledge was renamed is not enough. Because the deeper colonisation was never about facts — it was about feeling. It was about what happens to a people who are told, generation after generation, that their way of being in the world is less than.

The next chapter turns inward — to the psychology of a culture that learned to hesitate before claiming its own pride.
